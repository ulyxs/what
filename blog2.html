WHAT ARE INFORMATION SYSTEMS?

The system that collects, stores, manages, and processes data to transform raw inputs into meaningful information for businesses to use and rely on.

  • Data – the collection of raw, unprocessed observations, numbers, words, knowledge, or other information that may not yet make much sense (Badman & Kosinski, 2024).
  • Information – data that has been processed, analyzed, and interpreted, infused with meaning and value (Sheldon, 2024)

What is an Information System?

An information system is a set of interrelated components, including hardware, software, and telecommunications networks, that gather, analyze, store, and disseminate data to support individuals in managing organizations and making informed decisions (UC Berkeley School of Information, 2022; The Editors of Encyclopaedia Britannica, n.d.; Bourgeois, 2019)

I. Components of Information Systems (UC Berkeley, 2022; Bourgeois, 2019)

Hardware Hardware is the tangible part that humans can directly interact with, used to access and distribute data. Examples include computers, tablets, and phones
Software Software is a set of instructions and commands that direct the hardware to perform tasks or process information. It enables the computer to interpret and respond to human interaction with the hardware.
Database A database is a system that lets people index and organize data for easy access and specific use. It helps users analyze information and make decisions that improve organizational performance.
Network Without communication, information systems would not exist. A network is an interconnected system that enables computers to communicate and exchange information, such as files and documents.
People People are a vital part of information systems, although often overlooked in comparison to the technological components. They operate the systems to process data and access information, making work more efficient and productive.

II. Information & Communication Technology (ICT)

ICT refers to the infrastructure and devices that enable modern computing. These components enable people to create, share, and process information. When combined, they allow individuals and organizations to engage with the connected world (Kirvan et al., 2025; Fundación MAPFRE, 2024)

  • Internet – allows billions of people to access information and resources anytime and anywhere
  • Computers – a device that connects to the internet and gives users access to the world of information
  • Cloud-based Storage – a place where data is stored and accessed digitally, allowing users to retrieve it anytime through an internet connection without relying on a local device

III. Types of Information Systems (Florida Institute of Technology, 2024)

Information Systems are most commonly used in business organizations, where they can be represented through a pyramid model.

  • The pyramid model classifies different types of information systems according to their purpose and links them to tasks and responsibilities, depending on how users apply them in their roles within an organization.
Operational Level Transaction Processing System (TPS)
Records automated or semi-automated transactions between employees, customers, suppliers, or departments within the organization
  • Payroll Systems
  • Order Processing Systems
  • Reservation Systems
Middle Management Level Management Information System (MIS)
Helps ensure the smooth running of an organization in the short to medium term by allowing managers to evaluate performance through comparisons of current and past outputs
  • Sales Management Systems
  • Human Resource Management Systems
Decision Support System (DSS)
Analyzes structured information and helps managers project the potential effects of their decisions
  • Group Decision Support Systems
  • Financial Systems
Knowledge Management System (KMS)
These systems gather and use an organization’s shared knowledge to make work more efficient. They rely on a knowledge base and provide a central place to store information and access it easily (IBM, n.d.)
  • Document Management Systems
  • Knowledge Bases and Documentation
Executive Live Executive Information System (EIS)
A highly individualistic system that helps executives of an organization analyze internal and external information, spot long-term trends, and plan strategies. They are easy to use, customizable, and provide data from both the organization and outside sources.
  • Executive Information Systems are usually tailored to individual needs and often customized for specific groups of users.

IV. Analysis

When we encounter large amounts of data, it can be difficult to understand how they may be useful in our lives. The sheer volume of information from our daily experiences can feel overwhelming as we try to process and assign meaning to as much of it as possible. In doing so, we may struggle to distinguish what is truly important from what is redundant. This is why we need effective solutions to identify and focus on what matters most.

Information systems are among the best tools for managing large amounts of data and transforming them into valuable information. By converting raw data into meaningful information, these systems filter out redundancy and highlight what is truly useful. They also allow us to store and organize information in ways that are easy to understand and retrieve. While information systems are widely used in business operations, they can also be applied in many personal contexts.

For example, I create simple systems to keep track of assignments by categorizing tasks into different columns and assigning priorities. I also use them to monitor my productivity statistics, turning task data into insights about how quickly I complete work and how much time I spend on each task. This helps me identify areas for improvement and prepare myself better for future situations.

However, information systems can become overwhelming if not used properly, adding more clutter instead of clarity. When creating a system, it is important to set clear objectives, since combining too many functions can cause overlap and lead to confusing or useless information. To avoid this, every system should have a well-defined purpose and a clear way of being used.
